Introduction

Musculoskeletal teamwork

Movement happens when bones act as levers, joints act as pivots, and muscles pull on bones using tendons. A chicken wing is an excellent model for learning this because it has the same basic parts found in human arms: the humerus, radius, ulna, and hinge-like joints moved by pairs of muscles.

Why study movement with a chicken wing?

  • It clearly shows how antagonistic muscle pairs (like biceps and triceps) cause flexion and extension.
  • It reveals the roles of tendons (connect muscle to bone) and ligaments (connect bone to bone).
  • It helps connect classroom learning to sports science, physiotherapy, and injury prevention.

Realâ€‘world applications

  1. Designing safer training plans in PE and sports by understanding joint limits.
  2. Physiotherapy and rehabilitation after sprains, strains, or fractures.
  3. Robotics and prosthetics: copying lever systems and tendon-like cables.
  4. Veterinary practice: comparing limb function across animals.

Learning outcomes

You will be able to:
  • Label key bones of a chicken wing: humerus, radius, ulna.
  • Describe hinge joints and the role of cartilage and synovial fluid.
  • Explain how antagonistic muscle pairs cause movement.
  • Identify the roles of tendons and ligaments.
  • Relate levers (effort, load, fulcrum) to limb movement.

Summary

Quick recap
  • Bones act as levers; joints act as pivots; muscles apply pulling forces through tendons.
  • Antagonistic pairs (flexor/extensor) create controlled movement in one plane at hinge joints.
  • Ligaments stabilise joints; cartilage and synovial fluid reduce friction and wear.
  • Wing anatomy mirrors human arm function (humerus, radius, ulna).
